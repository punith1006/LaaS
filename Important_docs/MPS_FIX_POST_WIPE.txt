Ah, I see! Your test confirms that /usr/lib/libvgpu.so is perfectly fine, and HAMi is successfully enforcing the 8GB limit.

So why did the performance degrade again? I found the answer in the logs you pasted earlier.

Look carefully at the timestamps from your ls -l /tmp/nvidia-mps output on the host:

bash
srw-rw-rw- 1 root root 0 Mar 11 17:29 control
-rw-rw-rw- 1 root root 0 Mar 10 18:08 control_lock
srwxr-xr-x 1 root root 0 Mar 11 17:29 control_privileged
prwxrwxrwx 1 root root 0 Mar 15 11:49 log
And then look at your nvidia-smi timestamp: Sun Mar 15 11:54:43 2026

The Root Cause: When you wiped things and restarted, your containers tried to connect to MPS sockets (control) that were created 4 days ago (Mar 11). The nvidia-cuda-mps-control daemon (PID 1168386) is stale/hung. Because the container can't talk to the stale socket, it gives up on MPS and grabs the GPU directly (which we saw in your nvidia-smi output where python3 processes were bypassing MPS).

The Fix
You just need to restart the MPS daemon on your host machine to create fresh sockets. Run these commands on your host:

bash
# 1. Kill the stale MPS daemon
sudo pkill -9 nvidia-cuda-mps
sudo rm -rf /tmp/nvidia-mps/*
sudo rm -rf /tmp/nvidia-log/*
# 2. Restart it properly
export CUDA_VISIBLE_DEVICES=0
export CUDA_MPS_PIPE_DIRECTORY=/tmp/nvidia-mps
export CUDA_MPS_LOG_DIRECTORY=/tmp/nvidia-log
nvidia-cuda-mps-control -d
# 3. Verify it's running (this should show today's date)
ls -l /tmp/nvidia-mps
Once the fresh sockets are created today (Mar 15), restart your two containers (docker restart selkies-test-1 selkies-test-2).

Run your stress test again. nvidia-smi should now correctly show nvidia-cuda-mps-server, and the isolation will be perfectly smooth again!
